A simple guide to understanding the basics of cyber safety.

1) What is cyber safety and why is it important for students?

Cyber safety refers to the practice of protecting yourself, your information, and your devices from online threats like hackers, cyberbullying, viruses, and scams. Itâ€™s important for students because they often use the internet for studies, entertainment, and socializing. By being aware of cyber safety, students can avoid falling victim to online threats, protect their personal data, and use the internet responsibly and safely.

2) What kind of information do we need to protect online?

We must protect our personal details like names, passwords, photos, school information, phone numbers, and financial data. Even small details can be misused if they fall into the wrong hands.

3) Why should we avoid sharing personal information on social media?

Sharing personal information on social media (like your full name, address, phone number, school name, etc.) can make you vulnerable to cyber threats such as identity theft, cyberbullying, or even stalking. Cybercriminals can misuse this information to steal your identity or harm you in various ways. It's always safer to keep personal details private and share only what's necessary and safe.

4) How do these online threats actually affect us in real life?

Cyber threats can lead to hacked accounts, money loss, leaked photos or messages, damaged reputation, or emotional stress. What happens online doesnâ€™t stay online â€” it can impact school, friendships, and daily life.

5) What simple habits can help us stay safe on the internet every day?

Using strong passwords, avoiding suspicious links, keeping devices updated, turning on two-factor authentication, and thinking before sharing personal information are some easy habits that make a big difference.

6) How can we report concerns or get help if something feels unsafe online?

If you face cyberbullying, scams, or suspicious activity, you can talk to a trusted adult, report it to school authorities, or use national helplines and cybercrime portals. Asking for help is a responsible step â€” not a weakness.
